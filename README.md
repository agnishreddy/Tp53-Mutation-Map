# TP53 Mutation Atlas

An integrative bioinformatics visualization project that maps the **canonical human TP53 protein (393 aa)** across DNA codons, mRNA codons, amino acid sequence, structural domains, and clinically significant mutation hotspots.

## Project Overview

TP53 is one of the most frequently mutated tumor suppressor genes in human cancer. While many resources list TP53 variants, they are often fragmented across databases, papers, and protein annotation tools. This project brings those layers together into a single mutation-centric atlas.

The visualization connects:
- DNA codons.
- mRNA codons.
- Amino acid positions.
- Functional domains.
- Structural motifs.
- DNA-contact residues.
- Zinc-binding residues.
- Clinically relevant hotspot mutations.

## What This Atlas Shows

### 1. Full TP53 sequence context
- Canonical human TP53 protein length: 393 amino acids.
- Codon-by-codon mapping from DNA to mRNA to amino acid.
- Position-wise visualization across the full protein.

### 2. Structural and functional annotation
- Intrinsically disordered N- and C-terminal regions.
- DNA-binding domain.
- Tetramerization domain.
- Zinc-binding site.
- DNA-contact residues.
- Major cancer hotspot regions.

### 3. Clinically significant hotspots
The atlas highlights major TP53 mutation hotspots such as:
- R175
- Y220
- G245
- R248
- R249
- R273
- R282
- R337

For each hotspot, the project captures:
- Wild-type DNA and mRNA codons.
- Common codon substitutions.
- Transition or transversion pattern.
- Missense change.
- Functional category, such as structural mutation, DNA-contact mutation, or tetramerization-associated mutation.
- Reported clinical relevance and associated cancer types.

## Why This Project Matters

This project is useful because it turns raw variant information into an interpretable biological context.

Instead of looking at a TP53 mutation as only a residue change, the atlas helps answer:
- Where is the mutation located in the protein?
- Does it affect structural stability?
- Does it alter DNA binding?
- Is it part of a known hotspot?
- Is it associated with loss of function, dominant negative behavior, or altered druggability?

This makes the resource relevant for:
- Cancer bioinformatics.
- Variant interpretation.
- Computational oncology.
- Mutation prioritization.
- Structural biology workflows.
- CADD and mutation-informed drug discovery.
